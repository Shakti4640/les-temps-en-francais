Le Subjonctif Plus-que-parfait

Literary — completed action in a subjunctive context

TENSE 16: LE SUBJONCTIF PLUS-QUE-PARFAIT (Pluperfect Subjunctive)

📦 SECTION SUMMARY

What it is: The rarest tense in French — a compound literary tense formed with avoir or être in the subjonctif imparfait + past participle. It expresses a completed/prior action in a subjunctive clause when the main verb is in a past tense (literary concordance), OR it serves as a literary substitute for the conditionnel passé.

When to USE it: (Recognition only — literary/archaic)

  • Literary concordance des temps: completed past action in a subjunctive clause after a past-tense main verb (literary replacement for subjonctif passé)
  • Literary replacement for conditionnel passé in independent clauses and si-clause constructions
  • The famous inverted concessive construction (eût-il su... = "had he known...")
  • Classic literature (Flaubert, Hugo, Proust, Saint-Exupéry), legal documents, very formal speeches

When NOT to use it: In any spoken French, in informal/standard writing, in everyday communication, in modern journalism. Modern French uses subjonctif passé or conditionnel passé instead.

Modern Equivalent: Subjonctif passé (for subjunctive uses) / Conditionnel passé (for conditional uses)

Formation: que + AVOIR or ÊTRE in subjonctif imparfait + past participle

Difficulty Level: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Expert — recognition only, the absolute rarest tense)

1️⃣ WHAT IS LE SUBJONCTIF PLUS-QUE-PARFAIT?

The subjonctif plus-que-parfait is the fourth and final subjunctive tense, the compound counterpart of the subjonctif imparfait, and arguably the single rarest verb form in the entire French language.

The Subjunctive Family — Complete Picture:

TenseTypeTime ReferenceRegister
Subjonctif présentSimplePresent/Future✅ Modern — everyday
Subjonctif passéCompoundCompleted/Prior✅ Modern — everyday
Subjonctif imparfaitSimpleSimultaneous past📚 Literary only
Subjonctif plus-que-parfaitCompoundCompleted/Prior past📚📚 ULTRA-literary

The Parallel Structure of All Compound Tenses:

Simple Tense+ Past Participle =Compound Tense
Présent (ai/suis)+ PP= Passé composé
Imparfait (avais/étais)+ PP= Plus-que-parfait
Passé simple (eus/fus)+ PP= Passé antérieur
Futur simple (aurai/serai)+ PP= Futur antérieur
Conditionnel présent (aurais/serais)+ PP= Conditionnel passé
Subjonctif présent (aie/sois)+ PP= Subjonctif passé
Subjonctif imparfait (eusse/fusse)+ PP= Subjonctif plus-que-parfait

💡 KEY INSIGHT: If you know the subjonctif imparfait of avoir and être (from Tense 15), you already know how to form this tense. It's simply: subj. imparfait of auxiliary + past participle. That's it!

Where Does It Sit in the Concordance System?

Literary sequence of tenses (concordance des temps):

MAIN CLAUSE (Past Tense)    →    SUBORDINATE (Subjunctive)
                                  ├─ Simultaneous/Future → Subj. Imparfait
                                  └─ Prior/Completed     → Subj. Plus-que-parfait ⬅️

MAIN CLAUSE (Present/Future) →   SUBORDINATE (Subjunctive)
                                  ├─ Simultaneous/Future → Subj. Présent
                                  └─ Prior/Completed     → Subj. Passé

📌 In modern French, the bottom two rows handle EVERYTHING. The top two rows are purely literary.

3️⃣ WHEN TO USE LE SUBJONCTIF PLUS-QUE-PARFAIT ✅

Use #1: Literary Concordance des Temps — Completed Prior Action After a Past Main Verb

This is the primary grammatical function: when the main clause is in a past tense, the subjunctive action is completed/prior to the main action, and the writer is following strict literary concordance.

The Full Concordance Table:

Main Clause TenseSubjunctive ActionModern (spoken)Literary (written)
Present/FutureSimultaneous/FutureSubj. présentSubj. présent
Present/FuturePrior/CompletedSubj. passéSubj. passé
PastSimultaneous/FutureSubj. présent ⚠️Subj. imparfait
PastPrior/CompletedSubj. passé ⚠️Subj. plus-que-parfait ⬅️

⚠️ In modern French, the subj. présent and subj. passé handle ALL situations, regardless of main clause tense.

Examples — Literary vs. Modern:

Example 1: REGRETTER (to regret)

RegisterFrenchEnglish
📚 LiteraryIl regretta qu'elle fût partie sans un mot.He regretted that she had left without a word.
💬 ModernIl a regretté qu'elle soit partie sans un mot.He regretted that she had left without a word.

📝 Main verb: regretta (passé simple) → literary concordance demands subj. plus-que-parfait for a completed prior action.

RegisterFrenchEnglish

💬 ModernElle craignait que son fils ait commis une erreur irréparable.She feared that her son had committed an irreparable error.

RegisterFrenchEnglish

Use #2: Literary Replacement for the CONDITIONNEL PASSÉ

🔴 THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SPECIAL USE — and the one you'll encounter most often in literature!

In literary French, the subjonctif plus-que-parfait can replace the conditionnel passé in TWO situations:

A) In the RESULT clause of an unreal past condition (Si-clause)

Structure: Si + plus-que-parfait, ... subj. plus-que-parfait (instead of conditionnel passé)

RegisterFrenchEnglish
📚 LiterarySi j'avais su, je n'eusse jamais accepté.If I had known, I would never have accepted.
💬 ModernSi j'avais su, je n'aurais jamais accepté.If I had known, I would never have accepted.
RegisterFrenchEnglish
📚 LiteraryS'il fût venu, nous eussions été heureux.If he had come, we would have been happy.
💬 ModernS'il était venu, nous aurions été heureux.If he had come, we would have been happy.

⚠️ DOUBLE LITERARY: In the second example above, BOTH clauses use the subj. plus-que-parfait — the si-clause too! This is the most literary construction possible:

  • Si + subj. plus-que-parfait, ... subj. plus-que-parfait
  • Instead of: Si + plus-que-parfait, ... conditionnel passé

More Examples of This Replacement:

📚 Literary💬 Modern EquivalentEnglish
Si elle eût parlé, tout eût changé.Si elle avait parlé, tout aurait changé.If she had spoken, everything would have changed.
Si nous eussions su la vérité, nous fussions partis.Si nous avions su la vérité, nous serions partis.If we had known the truth, we would have left.
S'il l'eût voulu, il eût pu devenir roi.S'il l'avait voulu, il aurait pu devenir roi.If he had wanted it, he could have become king.
Si vous l'eussiez vu, vous eussiez tremblé.Si vous l'aviez vu, vous auriez tremblé.If you had seen him, you would have trembled.

💡 RECOGNITION TIP: When you see eût/eussent + past participle or fût/fussent + past participle in a sentence WITHOUT que before it, it's functioning as a conditionnel passé, NOT as a subjunctive!

B) In Independent Clauses (expressing regret, wishes about the past)

📚 Literary💬 Modern EquivalentEnglish
Qui l'eût cru ?Qui l'aurait cru ?Who would have believed it?
Qui l'eût dit ?Qui l'aurait dit ?Who would have said it?
On eût dit un fantôme.On aurait dit un fantôme.One would have said (it was) a ghost.
J'eusse voulu la sauver.J'aurais voulu la sauver.I would have wanted to save her.
Nul ne l'eût reconnu.Personne ne l'aurait reconnu.Nobody would have recognized him.
Il eût été préférable de partir.Il aurait été préférable de partir.It would have been preferable to leave.
Elle eût pu devenir une grande artiste.Elle aurait pu devenir une grande artiste.She could have become a great artist.

💡 This is extremely common in literary French. Sentences like "Qui l'eût cru ?" and "On eût dit..." are almost fixed expressions that even educated modern speakers might recognize.

Use #3: The Famous Inverted Concessive Construction

This is the most elegant and recognizable use of the subjonctif plus-que-parfait — the inverted concessive, meaning "even if" / "had he..."

Structure: Auxiliary (subj. PQP) + SUBJECT + past participle, ...

(Subject-verb INVERSION, no "si" or "même si")

📚 Literary💬 Modern EquivalentEnglish
Eût-il su la vérité, il n'eût rien changé.Même s'il avait su la vérité, il n'aurait rien changé.Even had he known the truth, he would have changed nothing.
Eût-elle voulu fuir, elle n'eût pas pu.Même si elle avait voulu fuir, elle n'aurait pas pu.Even had she wanted to flee, she couldn't have.
Eussent-ils possédé tout l'or du monde, ils n'eussent pas été heureux.Même s'ils avaient possédé tout l'or du monde, ils n'auraient pas été heureux.Even had they possessed all the gold in the world, they wouldn't have been happy.
Fût-il arrivé plus tôt, il n'eût rien pu faire.Même s'il était arrivé plus tôt, il n'aurait rien pu faire.Even had he arrived earlier, he could have done nothing.
Eût-on prévenu les habitants, la catastrophe eût été inévitable.Même si on avait prévenu les habitants, la catastrophe aurait été inévitable.Even had the inhabitants been warned, the catastrophe would have been unavoidable.

🔴 COMPARE with Subj. Imparfait Inversion (Tense 15):

  • Fût-il riche, ... = Even if he were rich, ... (present hypothetical — subj. imparfait)
  • Eût-il su, ... = Even had he known, ... (past hypothetical — subj. plus-que-parfait)

The difference: subj. imparfait inversion = present/general unreal condition; subj. PQP inversion = past unreal condition.

Use #4: After AVANT QUE, JUSQU'À CE QUE, etc. (Literary Temporal Clauses)

📚 Literary💬 Modern EquivalentEnglish
Il resta immobile jusqu'à ce que le bruit eût cessé.Il est resté immobile jusqu'à ce que le bruit ait cessé.He remained still until the noise had stopped.
Avant que le soleil eût disparu, ils atteignirent le village.Avant que le soleil ait disparu, ils ont atteint le village.Before the sun had disappeared, they reached the village.
Il ne partit point avant qu'on lui eût donné la permission.Il n'est pas parti avant qu'on lui ait donné la permission.He didn't leave before he had been given permission.

4️⃣ WHEN NOT TO USE LE SUBJONCTIF PLUS-QUE-PARFAIT ❌

❌ Rule #1: NEVER in Spoken French

❌ Nobody says this✅ Say this instead
Je doutais qu'il eût fini.Je doutais qu'il ait fini.
Si j'eusse su...Si j'avais su...
Qui l'eût cru ?Qui l'aurait cru ?

⚠️ Using the subjonctif plus-que-parfait in conversation would sound absurdly pretentious — even more so than the subjonctif imparfait. People would think you're joking or being theatrical.

❌ Rule #2: NEVER in Modern Standard Writing

❌ Too literary✅ Standard written French
Il fallait qu'il eût terminé avant midi.Il fallait qu'il ait terminé avant midi.
Bien qu'elle eût souffert, elle souriait.Bien qu'elle ait souffert, elle souriait.

❌ Rule #3: NEVER When the Action is Simultaneous (Not Prior)

If the subjunctive action is happening at the same time as the main verb (not before it), use the subjonctif imparfait (literary) or subjonctif présent (modern):

❌ Wrong✅ CorrectWhy
Il voulait qu'elle eût parlé. (if simultaneous)Il voulait qu'elle parlât. (literary) / qu'elle parle. (modern)Simultaneous action = simple form, not compound

The subj. PQP is ONLY for actions completed BEFORE the main verb action.

❌ Rule #4: NEVER After SI in Modern French

❌ Ultra-literary✅ Standard
S'il eût parlé, tout eût changé.S'il avait parlé, tout aurait changé.

The subj. PQP replacing the plus-que-parfait in si-clauses is exclusively found in 19th-century and earlier literature.

9️⃣ TECHNIQUES & MEMORY TIPS 🧠

Technique 1: The "Two Functions" Framework

The subj. PQP has exactly TWO functions. Memorize them:

FUNCTION 1: TRUE SUBJUNCTIVE
  → After que, in subjunctive clauses
  → Literary replacement for subjonctif passé
  → "I doubted that he HAD DONE..."
  → SIGNAL: you'll see "que" before it

FUNCTION 2: FAKE CONDITIONAL
  → Replaces conditionnel passé
  → In si-clauses and independent clauses
  → "He WOULD HAVE DONE..."
  → SIGNAL: NO "que" before it

💡 Quick test: Is there a que before it?

  • Yes → It's functioning as a subjunctive (Function 1)
  • No → It's functioning as a conditionnel passé replacement (Function 2)

Technique 2: The Circumflex Spotter

When reading literature, the circumflex accent on eût and fût is your alert signal:

  • See eût + past participle → subj. plus-que-parfait!
  • See fût + past participle → subj. plus-que-parfait!
  • No circumflex (eut, fut) → passé simple or passé antérieur
eût parlé  = subj. PQP (literary conditionnel passé OR literary subjunctive)
eut parlé  = passé antérieur (literary "had spoken" in temporal clauses)

fût parti  = subj. PQP
fut parti  = passé antérieur

🔴 THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT RECOGNITION TIP FOR READING FRENCH LITERATURE! The tiny circumflex (^) completely changes the meaning.

Technique 3: The Conversion Method

When you encounter the subj. PQP while reading, mentally convert it:

Step 1: Identify the auxiliary (eusse/eusses/eût/eussions/eussiez/eussent or fusse/fusses/fût/fussions/fussiez/fussent)

Step 2: Ask — is there que before it?

  • YES → Convert to subjonctif passé (aie/aies/ait/ayons/ayez/aient or sois/sois/soit/soyons/soyez/soient + PP)
  • NO → Convert to conditionnel passé (aurais/aurais/aurait/aurions/auriez/auraient or serais... + PP)

Example:

"S'il eût su..." → No que → = "S'il avait su..." (plus-que-parfait in si-clause)
"...qu'il eût su..." → Has que → = "...qu'il ait su..." (subjonctif passé)

Technique 4: The "Fixed Expressions" Shortcut

These quasi-fixed expressions appear even in moderately formal writing:

ExpressionMeaningYou might encounter it in...
Qui l'eût cru ?Who would have believed it?Essays, journalism
Qui l'eût dit ?Who would have said it?Essays, journalism
On eût dit...One would have said... / It looked like...Novels, descriptions
Eût-il su...Had he known...Formal speeches
Il eût été + adj.It would have been + adj.Legal, academic
Encore eût-il fallu que...Still, it would have been necessary that...Academic, legal

Memorize these few expressions and you'll recognize the subj. PQP most of the time!

Technique 5: Building From What You Know

If you know:       j'aurais parlé (conditionnel passé)
Then you know:     j'eusse parlé (subj. PQP = literary equivalent)

If you know:       que j'aie parlé (subjonctif passé)
Then you know:     que j'eusse parlé (subj. PQP = literary equivalent)

If you know:       je serais parti (conditionnel passé)
Then you know:     je fusse parti (subj. PQP = literary equivalent)

If you know:       que je sois parti (subjonctif passé)
Then you know:     que je fusse parti (subj. PQP = literary equivalent)

It's always the same past participle — only the auxiliary form changes!

The Subjunctive "Replacement" Map:

IN MODERN FRENCH:
  Subj. Présent  →  handles BOTH present subj. AND replaces subj. imparfait
  Subj. Passé    →  handles BOTH completed subj. AND replaces subj. plus-que-parfait

IN LITERARY FRENCH:
  Subj. Présent     →  present/future subj. after present/future main verb
  Subj. Passé       →  completed subj. after present/future main verb
  Subj. Imparfait   →  present/future subj. after past main verb
  Subj. PQP         →  completed subj. after past main verb
                        + literary conditionnel passé replacement
                        + inverted concessive (eût-il su...)
