TENSE 20: LE PARTICIPE PASSÉ (Past Participle)

📦 SECTION SUMMARY

What it is: The past participle is the single most important verb form in French after the infinitive and present tense. It is the essential building block of ALL compound tenses (passé composé, plus-que-parfait, futur antérieur, conditionnel passé, etc.), the passive voice, and is widely used as an adjective. It's the form you see after avoir/être in every compound tense: j'ai parlé, elle est partie, nous avions fini.

When to USE it: In ALL compound tenses (with avoir/être), as an adjective (une porte fermée), in the passive voice (La lettre a été écrite), in absolute constructions (Le travail terminé, il est parti), after certain verbs (Je l'ai vu tomber → Je l'ai vu tombé).

The BIG challenge: AGREEMENT RULES — when and how the past participle agrees in gender and number. This is considered the single most complex grammar topic in the French language.

Formation: Regular: -er → -é, -ir → -i, -re → -u (+ many irregulars)

Difficulty Level: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Formation is easy; AGREEMENT is the hardest grammar point in French)

1️⃣ WHAT IS LE PARTICIPE PASSÉ?

The participe passé (past participle) is a non-finite verb form that serves three major functions:

FunctionExampleEnglish
Building block of compound tensesJ'ai parlé. / Elle est partie.I spoke. / She left.
AdjectiveUne porte fermée.A closed door.
Passive voiceLa lettre a été écrite par Marie.The letter was written by Marie.

Why Is It So Important?

The participe passé appears in EVERY compound tense we've studied:

3️⃣ THE AGREEMENT RULES — THE HEART OF THIS SECTION

🔴 This is considered THE most complex grammar topic in French. Even native French speakers make mistakes with past participle agreement. Master this, and you've conquered the hardest part of French grammar.

Overview: The Three Agreement Scenarios

ScenarioRuleExample
With ÊTREAgrees with the SUBJECTElle est partie.
With AVOIRAgrees with the PRECEDING DIRECT OBJECT (if there is one)La lettre que j'ai écrite...
As an ADJECTIVEAgrees with the NOUN it modifiesUne porte fermée.

🔷 RULE 2: Agreement with AVOIR — Agrees with PRECEDING Direct Object

This is the trickiest rule in French grammar:

With AVOIR, the past participle agrees with the DIRECT OBJECT — but ONLY if the direct object comes BEFORE the verb.

When Does the Direct Object Come BEFORE?

There are exactly three situations:

SituationStructureExample
1. Direct object pronoun (le, la, les, l', me, te, nous, vous)Pronoun + avoir + PPJe l'ai vue. (I saw her.)
2. Relative pronoun QUENoun + que + subject + avoir + PPLa lettre que j'ai écrite...
3. Question with QUEL/COMBIENQuelle/Combien de + noun + avoir + PPQuelle route as-tu prise ?

🔴 THE KEY PRINCIPLE: With avoir, look for the direct object. Is it BEFORE the verb? If yes → agree. If no → don't agree. If there's no direct object at all → don't agree.

⚠️ No Agreement with INDIRECT Objects!

The past participle NEVER agrees with an indirect object (even if it precedes the verb):

FrenchEnglishWhy NO agreement?
Je leur ai parlé.I spoke to them.leur = INDIRECT object (parler à)
Elle **m'**a téléphoné.She called me.me = INDIRECT (téléphoner à)
La femme à qui j'ai parlé...The woman I spoke to...qui after à = INDIRECT
Je lui ai écrit.I wrote to him/her.lui = INDIRECT (écrire à)
Nous leur avons obéi.We obeyed them.leur = INDIRECT (obéir à)

💡 How to tell: If the verb takes à before its object (parler à, téléphoner à, écrire à, obéir à, plaire à, ressembler à), the object is INDIRECT → no agreement.

⚠️ No Agreement with EN

When the preceding direct object is the pronoun en, there is no agreement (according to modern standard grammar):

FrenchEnglishWhy NO agreement?
Des pommes ? J'en ai mangé.Apples? I ate some.en = no agreement
Des erreurs ? Il en a fait.Mistakes? He made some.en = no agreement
Des livres ? J'en ai lu trois.Books? I read three.en = no agreement

⚠️ Some traditional grammarians DO make the agreement with en (J'en ai mangées), but modern standard grammar says NO agreement. Both can be found in published texts.

🔷 RULE 3: Agreement with REFLEXIVE VERBS (Pronominal Verbs)

Reflexive verbs use être as their auxiliary, but the agreement rule is more complex than regular être verbs:

The past participle agrees with the REFLEXIVE PRONOUN — but ONLY if the reflexive pronoun is a DIRECT object.

Step-by-Step Method:

Step 1: Identify the reflexive pronoun (me, te, se, nous, vous, se)
Step 2: Ask: Is this pronoun a DIRECT object or INDIRECT object?
Step 3: If DIRECT → AGREE with the subject
         If INDIRECT → NO agreement

How to Determine Direct vs. Indirect:

Ask the question "WHOM?" or "WHAT?" after the verb:

  • If the answer is the reflexive pronoun → it's DIRECT → agree
  • If the verb has ANOTHER direct object → the reflexive pronoun is INDIRECT → no agreement

Category A: Reflexive Pronoun = DIRECT Object → AGREE ✅

These are verbs where you do the action to yourself:

💡 How to recognize: If you can't use the verb without se (there's no souvenir, enfuir, évanouir without se), it's essentially pronominal → always agree.

⚠️ THE NOTORIOUS EXCEPTION: SE FAIRE + INFINITIVE

Elle s'est fait couper les cheveux. (She had her hair cut.)

With se faire + infinitive, the past participle fait is INVARIABLE (never agrees) according to the 1990 spelling reform and modern grammar:

  • Elle s'est fait opérer. ✅ (NOT faite)
  • Elles se sont fait insulter. ✅ (NOT faites)
  • Elle s'est fait voler son sac. ✅ (NOT faite)

💡 This was officially confirmed by the Académie française. Before 1990, some grammarians required agreement (elle s'est faite couper...), but the modern rule is simpler: fait + infinitive = invariable.

⚠️ SE LAISSER + INFINITIVE

Similar to se faire, se laisser before an infinitive follows the same principle in modern grammar:

FrenchEnglish
Elle s'est laissé convaincre.She let herself be convinced.
Elles se sont laissé emporter.They let themselves be carried away.

Modern grammar: laissé + infinitive = invariable (though older texts may show agreement).

🔷 RULE 4: Special Cases and Exceptions

A) Impersonal Verbs → NEVER Agree

FrenchEnglishWhy?
Les orages qu'il y a eu...The storms there were...Impersonal il → no agreement
La chaleur qu'il a fait...The heat it was...Impersonal il fait → no agreement
Les heures qu'il a fallu...The hours it took...Impersonal il faut → no agreement
La neige qu'il a neigé...The snow that fell...Impersonal il neige → no agreement

B) Verbs of Measure/Cost/Weight → NO Agreement When Expressing Quantity

FrenchEnglishWhy?
Les deux heures que le film a duré...The two hours the film lasted...deux heures = measure, not true DO
Les 80 kilos que Pierre a pesé...The 80 kilos Pierre weighed...80 kilos = measure
Les 200 euros que ce livre a coûté...The 200 euros this book cost...200 euros = measure
Les kilomètres que nous avons marché...The kilometers we walked...kilomètres = measure

⚠️ BUT if these verbs are used with a TRUE direct object (figurative meaning), agreement applies:

  • Les efforts que ce projet m'a coûtés... (The efforts this project cost me...) → figurative = agree
  • Les dangers qu'il a courus... (The dangers he ran...) → figurative = agree

C) Past Participle Followed by an INFINITIVE

This is a complex area with specific rules:

With FAIRE + infinitive → ALWAYS invariable:

FrenchRule
La maison que j'ai fait construire.fait + infinitive = invariable
Les gâteaux que j'ai fait préparer.fait + infinitive = invariable

With other verbs + infinitive → agree IF the preceding DO is the subject of the infinitive:

FrenchEnglishAgree?Why?
La chanteuse que j'ai entendue chanter.The singer I heard sing.✅ YesLa chanteuse performs the singing (subject of infinitive)
La chanson que j'ai entendu chanter.The song I heard (being) sung.❌ NoLa chanson doesn't sing — it's sung (object of infinitive)
Les enfants que j'ai vus jouer.The children I saw play.✅ YesLes enfants perform the playing

💡 The test: Does the preceding noun DO the action of the infinitive?

  • YES → Agree: La fille que j'ai vue danser (she dances)
  • NO → Don't agree: La chanson que j'ai entendu chanter (the song doesn't sing)

⚠️ PRACTICAL NOTE: This rule is so subtle that many native speakers don't apply it consistently. In everyday writing, not making this agreement is rarely noticed.

9️⃣ TECHNIQUES & MEMORY TIPS 🧠

Technique 1: The "Three Questions" Method for AVOIR Agreement

Whenever you have avoir + past participle, ask these three questions in order:

Question 1: Is there a DIRECT OBJECT?
  → NO  → No agreement. STOP. (J'ai dormi. J'ai parlé.)
  → YES → Go to Question 2.

Question 2: Does the direct object come BEFORE the verb?
  → NO  → No agreement. STOP. (J'ai mangé une pomme.)
  → YES → Go to Question 3.

Question 3: What is the gender and number of that direct object?
  → Apply agreement accordingly.
     (La pomme que j'ai mangéE. Les films que j'ai vuS.)

Technique 2: The "Body Parts" Rule for Reflexives

Reflexive verb + body part = NO agreement

This covers the vast majority of "reflexive with another DO" cases:

se laver les mains     → Elle s'est LAVÉ les mains.     ❌ no agreement
se brosser les dents   → Elle s'est BROSSÉ les dents.   ❌ no agreement
se casser la jambe     → Elle s'est CASSÉ la jambe.      ❌ no agreement
se couper le doigt     → Elle s'est COUPÉ le doigt.      ❌ no agreement
se tordre la cheville  → Elle s'est TORDU la cheville.   ❌ no agreement
se brûler la main      → Elle s'est BRÛLÉ la main.       ❌ no agreement
se faire mal au dos    → Elle s'est FAIT mal au dos.     ❌ no agreement

BUT without the body part:

se laver               → Elle s'est LAVÉE.               ✅ agreement!
se couper              → Elle s'est COUPÉE.              ✅ agreement!

💡 Memory rule: If there's a body part after the reflexive verb, the body part "steals" the direct object role from se, making se indirect → no agreement.

Technique 3: The "À = No Agreement" Rule for Reflexives

If the non-reflexive version of the verb takes À → no agreement in reflexive form

parler À quelqu'un    → Elles se sont PARLÉ.      ❌ (parler à)
téléphoner À          → Elles se sont TÉLÉPHONÉ.  ❌ (téléphoner à)
sourire À             → Elles se sont SOURI.      ❌ (sourire à)
plaire À              → Elles se sont PLU.        ❌ (plaire à)
mentir À              → Ils se sont MENTI.        ❌ (mentir à)
nuire À               → Ils se sont NUI.          ❌ (nuire à)
ressembler À          → Elles se sont RESSEMBLÉ.  ❌ (ressembler à)
succéder À            → Ils se sont SUCCÉDÉ.      ❌ (succéder à)

💡 Quick test: Can you say "verb + quelqu'un" directly (without à)?

  • Laver quelqu'un ✅ → se is direct → AGREE: Elle s'est lavée.
  • Parler quelqu'un ❌ (you need parler à quelqu'un) → se is indirect → NO agreement: Elles se sont parlé.

Technique 4: The Irregular PP "Ending Groups" Memory System

Organize irregular past participles by their ending sound:

Sound /e/:  -É    → allé, né, été
Sound /i/:  -I    → dormi, parti, sorti, suivi, ri, suffi
            -IS   → pris, mis, acquis, assis
            -IT   → dit, écrit, fait, conduit, cuit
Sound /y/:  -U    → eu, bu, cru, dû, lu, pu, su, vu, voulu, venu, tenu,
                     vécu, connu, couru, reçu, battu, valu, fallu, plu,
                     résolu, conclu, vaincu
Sound /ɛʁ/: -ERT  → ouvert, offert, souffert, couvert, découvert
Sound /ɛ̃/:  -INT  → craint, peint, éteint, atteint, joint

Technique 5: The "Compound Ending" Families

PRENDRE family → always -PRIS:
  pris, appris, compris, surpris, entrepris

METTRE family → always -MIS:
  mis, permis, promis, admis, soumis, commis

Technique 6: The "Preceding DO Spotter"

Train yourself to spot preceding direct objects by looking for these three patterns:

Pattern 1: PRONOUN before avoir
  "Je LA vois" → "Je l'ai vuE"
  "Je LES mange" → "Je les ai mangéS/éES"

  "La lettre QUE j'ai..." → agree with "la lettre" → écritE
  "Les films QUE nous avons..." → agree with "les films" → vuS

Pattern 3: QUEL/COMBIEN DE in questions
  "QUELLE robe as-tu..." → agree with "robe" → misE
  "COMBIEN DE livres a-t-il..." → agree with "livres" → luS

La catastrophe que j'avais prévu qu'il arriverait...The catastrophe I had predicted would happen...❌ No — que refers to a clause, not a gendered noun
Cette victoire, je l'avais espéré. (= j'avais espéré qu'on gagnerait)This victory, I had hoped for it.❌ No — l' refers to a clause/idea

⚠️ BUT if l' refers to a specific noun: Cette victoire, je l'avais espérée. → if you're referring to la victoire specifically → +e. This is a subtle distinction that even experts debate.

🔷 The "Preceding DO + Infinitive" Cases — Detailed

💡 The Universal Test: Ask: "Does the noun before que PERFORM the action of the infinitive?"

  • YES → The noun is the subject of the infinitive → AGREE
  • NO → The noun is the object of the infinitive → NO agreement

Exception: FAIT + infinitive → ALWAYS invariable, regardless: La maison que j'ai fait construire. ✅

🔷 Verbs That Can Use AVOIR or ÊTRE (with Different Meanings)

Some verbs use avoir for transitive meaning and être for intransitive meaning:

VerbWith AVOIR (transitive)With ÊTRE (intransitive)
monterJ'ai monté les valises. (I carried up the suitcases.)Je suis monté(e) au 3e étage. (I went up to the 3rd floor.)
descendreJ'ai descendu l'escalier. (I went down the stairs.)Je suis descendu(e) du bus. (I got off the bus.)
sortirJ'ai sorti le chien. (I took the dog out.)Je suis sorti(e). (I went out.)
rentrerJ'ai rentré la voiture. (I put the car in.)Je suis rentré(e) à la maison. (I came home.)
retournerJ'ai retourné la crêpe. (I flipped the crêpe.)Je suis retourné(e) en France. (I went back to France.)
passerJ'ai passé un examen. (I took an exam.)Je suis passé(e) chez toi. (I stopped by your place.)

💡 The Rule:

  • Has a direct object → use AVOIR (agreement with preceding DO only)
  • No direct object (movement/change of state) → use ÊTRE (agreement with subject)
