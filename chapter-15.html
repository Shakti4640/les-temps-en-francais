TENSE 15: LE SUBJONCTIF IMPARFAIT (Imperfect Subjunctive)

📦 SECTION SUMMARY

What it is: The subjonctif imparfait is a literary/formal past subjunctive tense used when the main clause is in a past tense. It replaces the subjonctif présent in formal written French to maintain the concordance des temps (sequence of tenses). It is the subjunctive equivalent of the imparfait de l'indicatif.

When to USE it: In literary novels, formal speeches, legal documents, academic writing, and historical texts — when the main clause is in a past tense (passé simple, imparfait, plus-que-parfait, conditionnel) and the subordinate clause requires the subjunctive for a simultaneous or future action relative to that past moment.

When NOT to use it: In everyday speech, informal writing, emails, text messages, normal conversation — modern French uses the subjonctif présent in ALL registers for most situations. Using the subjonctif imparfait in casual speech will sound absurd, pretentious, or comical.

Formation: Based on the passé simple — take the passé simple tu form, remove the -s, and add specific endings.

Difficulty Level: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Advanced — but only for RECOGNITION, not production in most cases)

Bottom Line: You need to recognize it when reading literature. You almost certainly will never need to produce it in daily life.

🧠 THE CONCEPT: WHY DOES THIS TENSE EXIST?

The Problem: Sequence of Tenses (La Concordance des Temps)

French has a rule (especially in formal/literary writing) that the tense of the subordinate clause should match the tense of the main clause. This is called la concordance des temps.

In the indicative mood, this works naturally:

Main Clause TenseSubordinate Clause TenseExample
PresentPresentJe sais qu'il vient. (I know he's coming.)
PastImparfaitJe savais qu'il venait. (I knew he was coming.)
PresentPassé composéJe sais qu'il est venu. (I know he came.)
PastPlus-que-parfaitJe savais qu'il était venu. (I knew he had come.)

But what about the subjunctive mood? In modern French, we only have TWO subjunctive tenses in everyday use:

Modern SubjunctiveExample
Subjonctif Présentque je fasse
Subjonctif Passéque j'aie fait

This means that in modern French, we use the subjonctif présent regardless of whether the main clause is present OR past:

Modern French (acceptable everywhere)
Je veux qu'il vienne. (present + subj. présent ✅)
Je voulais qu'il vienne. (past + subj. présent ✅ in modern French)

But in classical/literary French, this felt wrong. If the main clause was in the past, the subjunctive should ALSO be in the past. So they used:

Literary French (formal concordance)
Je veux qu'il vienne. (present + subj. présent ✅)
Je voulais qu'il vînt. (past + subj. IMPARFAIT ✅ in literary French)

💡 THE KEY INSIGHT: The subjonctif imparfait is to the subjonctif présent what the imparfait is to the présent — it's the past tense version used when the main clause is in the past.

The Full Concordance des Temps Table (Literary French):

Main ClauseSubordinate ActionLiterary SubjunctiveModern Subjunctive
Present / FutureSimultaneous / FutureSubj. PrésentSubj. Présent
Present / FuturePrior / CompletedSubj. PasséSubj. Passé
PastSimultaneous / Future (at that past time)Subj. Imparfait 📚Subj. Présent
PastPrior / Completed (before that past time)Subj. Plus-que-parfait 📚Subj. Passé

📚 = Literary only

In other words:

  • Modern French uses 2 subjunctive tenses (présent + passé) for everything
  • Literary French uses 4 subjunctive tenses (présent + passé + imparfait + plus-que-parfait) following strict concordance

Visual Timeline:

LITERARY CONCORDANCE:

Main clause in PRESENT:
├── Simultaneous/Future → Subj. PRÉSENT (que je fasse)
└── Prior/Completed → Subj. PASSÉ (que j'aie fait)

Main clause in PAST:
├── Simultaneous/Future → Subj. IMPARFAIT (que je fisse) 📚
└── Prior/Completed → Subj. PLUS-QUE-PARFAIT (que j'eusse fait) 📚

MODERN USAGE:

Main clause in PRESENT or PAST:
├── Simultaneous/Future → Subj. PRÉSENT (que je fasse)
└── Prior/Completed → Subj. PASSÉ (que j'aie fait)

Why It Sounds Funny Today

The subjonctif imparfait has fallen out of use because many of its forms sound awkward, archaic, or even comical to modern ears:

VerbSubj. ImparfaitWhy It Sounds Funny
aimerqu'il aimâtThe â sounds very old-fashioned
venirqu'il vîntSounds like vin (wine)
faireque nous fissionsSounds unusual
pouvoirqu'il pûtSounds like pu (past participle) with a hat accent
avoirque nous eussionsVery complex sound cluster
êtreque vous fussiezAlmost comical
savoirqu'il sûtSounds archaic
devoirqu'il dûtEasily confused with dû (past participle)
croireque nous crussionsVery unusual sound

😄 Famous French joke: A teacher asks a student to conjugate aimer in the subjonctif imparfait. The student says: "Que j'aimasse, que tu aimasses, qu'il aimât..." and the whole class laughs. It's the -asse ending that sounds like a certain body part in French slang! This is one reason the tense died out in speech.

1️⃣ FORMATION (HOW TO CONJUGATE)

The Master Formula

Step 1: Take the PASSÉ SIMPLE form of tu
Step 2: Remove the final -s
Step 3: Add the subjonctif imparfait endings

💡 THIS IS WHY KNOWING THE PASSÉ SIMPLE IS ESSENTIAL! The subjonctif imparfait is entirely built on the passé simple stem.

The Endings:

SubjectEnding
que je-sse
que tu-sses
qu'il/elle-^t (same as passé simple stem + circumflex accent)
que nous-ssions
que vous-ssiez
qu'ils/elles-ssent

⚠️ THE CIRCUMFLEX ACCENT: The il/elle form ALWAYS has a circumflex accent (^) over the vowel before the -t. This is the most recognizable feature of the subjonctif imparfait!

  • qu'il aimât (â)
  • qu'il finît (î)
  • qu'il vînt (î)
  • qu'il eût (û)
  • qu'il fût (û)

  • Passé simple has i (finit) → Subj. imp. has i (finît)
  • Passé simple has u (fut) → Subj. imp. has u (fût)

2️⃣ WHEN TO USE THE SUBJONCTIF IMPARFAIT ✅

The Core Rule:

Main clause in a PAST TENSE + subjunctive trigger + simultaneous/future action (at that past time)
→ SUBJONCTIF IMPARFAIT (in literary French)

Use #1: Main Clause in PASSÉ SIMPLE (Most Common Literary Context)

Modern French (subj. présent)Literary French (subj. imparfait)
Il voulut qu'elle vienne.Il voulut qu'elle vînt.
Il fallut qu'il parte.Il fallut qu'il partît.
Le roi ordonna qu'on fasse silence.Le roi ordonna qu'on fît silence.

Use #5: Independent / Main Clause Subjunctive (Wishes, Literary Style)

In very literary style, the subjonctif imparfait can appear in independent clauses expressing wishes or hypothetical situations:

Literary FrenchEnglish
Plût au ciel qu'il fût encore vivant !Would to heaven he were still alive!
Dût-il y perdre la vie, il n'abandonnerait pas.Even if he should lose his life, he would not give up.

Ne fût-ce que pour un instant...Were it only for an instant... / If only for a moment...

💡 These are extremely formal and literary. You'll find them in classic novels (Hugo, Flaubert, Balzac) and sometimes in very formal contemporary prose.

3️⃣ WHEN NOT TO USE THE SUBJONCTIF IMPARFAIT ❌

❌ Rule #1: NEVER in Everyday Speech

❌ Absurdly literary in speech✅ Normal modern French
Il faudrait que tu fusses plus gentil. 😂Il faudrait que tu sois plus gentil.
Je voulais que tu vinsses chez moi. 😂Je voulais que tu viennes chez moi.
Bien qu'il plût, nous sortîmes. 😂Bien qu'il pleuve, nous sommes sortis.

😄 Using the subjonctif imparfait in casual conversation would be like saying "Wouldst thou accompany me to the marketplace?" in English. People would stare, then laugh.

❌ Rule #2: NEVER in Informal Writing

❌ Out of place✅ Appropriate
Text message: "Faudrait que tu vinsses ce soir" 😂"Faudrait que tu viennes ce soir"
Email to friend: "Je voulais que tu susses que..." 😂"Je voulais que tu saches que..."
Social media: "Bien qu'il fût tard...""Bien qu'il soit tard..." OR "Même s'il était tard..."

❌ Rule #3: Even in Literature, Avoid the Most Awkward Forms

Even literary authors today tend to avoid the most ridiculous-sounding forms and find workarounds:

Avoided (too awkward even for literature)Preferred Alternative
que nous aimassionsRestructure the sentence to use il/elle form
que vous crussiezUse infinitive construction or different wording
que nous voulussionsRephrase entirely

💡 THE PRACTICAL RULE: Even in literary French, writers tend to use the subjonctif imparfait mainly in the 3rd person singular (qu'il fût, qu'il eût, qu'il vînt, qu'elle pût) and sometimes 3rd person plural. The 1st and 2nd person forms (que je parlasse, que nous eussions) are increasingly rare even in formal writing.

Pour que... (past context)...elle puisse comprendre....elle pût comprendre.
Sans que... (past context)...il le sache....il le sût.

💡 REMEMBER: In modern French (including formal modern writing like newspapers, academic papers, etc.), the subjonctif présent is perfectly acceptable even when the main clause is in the past. The subjonctif imparfait is reserved for deliberately literary or archaic style.

6️⃣ FAMOUS LITERARY EXAMPLES

From Victor Hugo (Les Misérables):

"Bien qu'il fût évêque, ou plutôt parce qu'il était évêque, il n'avait rien changé au mobilier de la maison."

(Although he was a bishop, or rather because he was a bishop, he had changed nothing in the furnishings of the house.)

"Il fallait que le voyageur eût bien faim pour s'arrêter dans un tel endroit."

(The traveler must have been very hungry to stop in such a place.)

From Gustave Flaubert (Madame Bovary):

"Elle souhaitait que son fils fût beau, fort et brun."

(She wished her son to be handsome, strong, and dark-haired.)

"Bien qu'il fît très chaud, elle gardait son châle sur les épaules."

(Although it was very hot, she kept her shawl on her shoulders.)

From Albert Camus (L'Étranger — notably AVOIDS the subj. imparfait!):

Camus deliberately used a modern, spare style. He wrote:

"J'ai pensé que c'était dimanche et que ça m'ennuyait."
(NOT: "que cela m'ennuyât")

This shows how even major 20th-century authors moved away from the subjonctif imparfait.

From Marcel Proust (À la recherche du temps perdu):

"Il eût fallu qu'elle pût le voir sans qu'il le sût."

(It would have been necessary for her to be able to see him without his knowing it.)

This sentence contains THREE literary subjunctive forms! (eût fallu = subj. plus-que-parfait, pût = subj. imparfait, sût = subj. imparfait)

From Stendhal (Le Rouge et le Noir):

"Avant que Julien pût s'en apercevoir, elle avait disparu."

(Before Julien could notice, she had disappeared.)

From Contemporary Formal Writing (Le Monde newspaper, occasional use):

"Le président souhaitait que la réforme fût adoptée avant l'été."

(The president wished the reform to be adopted before summer.)

💡 Even in formal journalism, this is increasingly rare. Most journalists today would write: "...que la réforme soit adoptée..."

7️⃣ THE CONFUSING LOOK-ALIKES

Trap #1: Subj. Imparfait vs. Passé Simple (il/elle form)

Passé SimpleSubjonctif ImparfaitHow to Tell?
il parlaqu'il parlâtCircumflex accent â + context after que
il finitqu'il finîtCircumflex accent î + context after que
il futqu'il fûtCircumflex accent û + context after que
il eutqu'il eûtCircumflex accent û + context after que
il vintqu'il vîntCircumflex accent î + context after que

💡 THE RULE: If it follows que and there's a circumflex accent, it's subjonctif imparfait. If it stands alone as a main verb, it's passé simple.

  • Il fut heureux. (passé simple — main verb)
  • ...bien qu'il fût heureux. (subj. imparfait — after bien que)

Trap #2: Subj. Imparfait vs. Subj. Présent (je/tu/nous/vous/ils forms of -IR verbs)

Subjonctif PrésentSubjonctif ImparfaitIdentical?
que je finisseque je finisse✅ IDENTICAL!
que tu finissesque tu finisses✅ IDENTICAL!
qu'il finissequ'il finît❌ Different (circumflex!)
que nous finissionsque nous finissions✅ IDENTICAL!
que vous finissiezque vous finissiez✅ IDENTICAL!
qu'ils finissentqu'ils finissent✅ IDENTICAL!

⚠️ For -IR verbs (and others with passé simple in -is), 5 out of 6 subjonctif imparfait forms are identical to the subjonctif présent! Only the il/elle form differs (circumflex).

This means context is ESSENTIAL for distinguishing them.

Trap #3: Subj. Imparfait vs. Imparfait de l'Indicatif (nous/vous forms)

Imparfait IndicatifSubj. ImparfaitIdentical?
nous parlionsque nous parlassions❌ Different
nous finissionsque nous finissions✅ IDENTICAL!
nous faisionsque nous fissions❌ Different
nous avionsque nous eussions❌ Different
nous étionsque nous fussions❌ Different

💡 The overlap between subj. imparfait and imparfait indicatif only occurs with -IR verbs (Group 2). For all other verbs, they're clearly different.

9️⃣ THE SUBJONCTIF IMPARFAIT IN CONDITIONAL SENTENCES (Si-Clauses) — VERY LITERARY

In extremely literary/classical French, the subjonctif imparfait (and plus-que-parfait) could replace the conditionnel in si-clause constructions:

Literary Si-Clause Substitutions:

Standard FrenchLiterary SubstitutionEnglish
Si j'avais de l'argent, j'achèterais une maison.Eussé-je de l'argent, j'achèterais une maison.If I had money, I would buy a house.
Si j'avais su, je ne serais pas venu.Eussé-je su, je ne serais pas venu.Had I known, I wouldn't have come.
Si c'était vrai...Fût-ce vrai...Were it true...
Même si c'était le roi...Fût-ce le roi...Were it even the king...

⚠️ This construction uses inversion (verb before subject) and is EXTREMELY literary. You'll encounter it in 19th-century novels and formal rhetoric, almost never in modern writing.

🔟 THE EVOLUTION: FROM OBLIGATORY TO EXTINCT

CenturyStatus of Subjonctif Imparfait
16th–17th centuryObligatory in formal and informal writing when main clause was past
18th centuryStill standard in all written French; beginning to fade from speech
19th centuryPeak literary usage (Hugo, Flaubert, Balzac, Stendhal); disappearing from speech
Early 20th centuryProust uses it extensively; Camus largely avoids it; becoming optional in writing
Late 20th centuryRestricted to very formal/literary contexts; 3rd person singular most common
21st centuryEssentially extinct in speech; rare in writing; mainly 3rd person qu'il fût/eût; seen as stylistic choice

💡 WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOU:

  • Reading classical literature (pre-1950)? → You'll encounter it constantly. MUST recognize it.
  • Reading modern literature (post-1950)? → Occasional, mainly fût/eût. Should recognize it.
  • Reading newspapers/academic texts? → Very rare. Nice to recognize.
  • Writing? → Almost never needed. Use subjonctif présent.
  • Speaking? → NEVER. People will think you're joking.

1️⃣1️⃣ TECHNIQUES & MEMORY TIPS 🧠

Technique 1: The "Passé Simple Bridge"

If you know the passé simple, you know the subjonctif imparfait!

Passé Simple (tu) → remove -s → add -se, -ses, -^t, -sions, -siez, -sent

Passé Simple (tu)→ Remove -s →→ Subj. Imp. (il)
tu parlasparla-qu'il parlât
tu finisfini-qu'il finît
tu fusfu-qu'il fût
tu vinsvin-qu'il vînt

Technique 2: The "Circumflex Spotlight"

When reading literature, the circumflex accent on a verb form after que is your SPOTLIGHT signal for the subjonctif imparfait:

  • See â + t after que → subj. imp. of an -ER verb (qu'il parlât)
  • See î + t after que → subj. imp. of an -IR/-IS verb (qu'il finît, qu'il vînt)
  • See û + t after que → subj. imp. of an -US verb (qu'il fût, qu'il eût, qu'il pût)

Technique 3: The "Top 5" Strategy

Don't try to memorize ALL forms. Just memorize the 5 most common forms you'll encounter:

#FormVerbYou'll See It After...
1fûtêtrebien qu'il fût... sans qu'il fût...
2eûtavoirbien qu'il eût... sans qu'il eût...
3pûtpouvoirpour qu'il pût... sans qu'il pût...
4fîtfaireil fallut qu'il fît...
5vîntveniravant qu'il vînt...

These 5 forms account for probably 80%+ of all subjonctif imparfait occurrences in literature!

Technique 4: The "Convert to Modern" Reading Strategy

When reading literature and you hit a subjonctif imparfait, mentally convert it:

  1. Spot the form: "...qu'il fût malade..."
  2. Identify the verb: fût = être
  3. Convert to subj. présent: "...qu'il soit malade..."
  4. Understand: "...that he was/be sick..."

Technique 5: The "Don't Panic" Approach

You will NEVER need to produce this tense in:

  • Conversation
  • Emails
  • Standard essays
  • Most exams (unless specifically on literary grammar)
  • Professional writing

You only need to RECOGNIZE it when reading:

  • French novels (especially pre-1960)
  • Formal/legal documents
  • Some academic texts
  • Some very formal journalism

1️⃣4️⃣ CULTURAL NOTES 🎭

When French People Use It Jokingly:

French people sometimes use the subjonctif imparfait humorously to sound pretentious, old-fashioned, or comically formal:

Humorous UsageContext
"Il faudrait que je fusse plus intelligent !"Self-deprecating joke
"Encore eût-il fallu que je le susse !"Famous humorous expression = "I would have had to know it!"
"Bien que je fusse en retard, je pris le temps de déjeuner."Telling a story in an exaggeratedly literary style

💡 The expression "Encore eût-il fallu que je le susse !" (or variations) has become almost a meme in French culture — it's used to mock overly formal language while simultaneously showing off grammatical knowledge.

The Académie Française's Position:

The Académie française (the official guardian of the French language) still technically considers the subjonctif imparfait to be "correct" French. However, even they acknowledge it's no longer required in most modern writing.

In French Education:

  • Students learn to recognize the subjonctif imparfait in middle/high school
  • They rarely have to produce it
  • It appears on the agrégation (highest teaching exam) and in literary analysis
  • Most French adults can recognize qu'il fût and qu'il eût but would struggle with forms like que nous voulussions

1️⃣5️⃣ FINAL COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

📦 EVERYTHING YOU NEED TO REMEMBER:

1. WHAT IT IS:

  • The past/literary form of the subjunctive, used when the main clause is in a past tense
  • Part of the concordance des temps (sequence of tenses) in formal/literary French

2. FORMATION:

  • Based on the passé simple (tu form) — remove -s, add endings
  • Three vowel groups: -a- (ER verbs), -i- (IR verbs/IS passé simple), -u- (US passé simple)
  • Circumflex accent on the il/elle form: -ât, -ît, -ût

3. WHEN IT'S USED:

  • Literary novels (especially pre-1960)
  • Very formal writing (legal, academic, rhetorical)
  • Main clause in a past tense + subjunctive trigger + simultaneous/future action

4. WHEN IT'S NOT USED:

  • NEVER in speech (sounds comical/pretentious)
  • Rarely in modern writing (subjonctif présent is universally accepted)
  • Even in literature, mainly the 3rd person singular survives

5. THE TOP 5 FORMS TO KNOW:

  • qu'il fût (être)
  • qu'il eût (avoir)
  • qu'il pût (pouvoir)
  • qu'il fît (faire)
  • qu'il vînt (venir)

6. PRACTICAL ADVICE:

  • RECOGNIZE it when reading → convert mentally to subj. présent
  • DON'T PRODUCE it unless writing deliberately literary prose
  • When in doubt → use subjonctif présent (always correct in modern French)
