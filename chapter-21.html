L'Infinitif Présent & Passé

After prepositions, other verbs, as a noun

TENSE 21: L'INFINITIF PRÉSENT & PASSÉ (Present & Past Infinitive) — THE GRAND FINALE 🎆

📦 SECTION SUMMARY

What it is: The infinitive is the base form of every French verb — the form found in dictionaries. The infinitif présent (parler, finir, vendre) is the most fundamental verb form in the language. The infinitif passé (avoir parlé, être parti) is its compound counterpart, expressing a completed action prior to another. Together, they are used constantly — after other verbs, after prepositions, as nouns, in commands on signs, and as building blocks of the entire verb system.

When to USE them: After other verbs (Je veux parler), after prepositions except en (sans parler, avant de parler, pour parler), as the subject of a sentence (Lire est un plaisir), for impersonal instructions (Ne pas fumer), after après (après avoir mangé), and in countless fixed expressions.

When NOT to use them: As the main/only verb of a sentence with a specific subject (Je parler français), after en (use gérondif: en parlant), when a conjugated verb is needed for a specific tense/mood.

The BIG challenge: Knowing which preposition to use (or not) between a verb and an infinitive: à, de, or nothing (commencer à, décider de, vouloir ∅).

Formation: Infinitif présent = dictionary form. Infinitif passé = avoir/être + past participle.

Difficulty Level: ⭐⭐⭐ (The form is simple; knowing WHEN to use it and WHICH preposition requires extensive vocabulary knowledge)

1️⃣ WHAT IS L'INFINITIF?

The infinitif (infinitive) is the unconjugated, base form of a verb. It's the form you look up in the dictionary, the form that names the action without specifying who does it or when.

The Four Infinitive Groups:

🔷 NEGATION OF THE INFINITIVE

Both negative words go BEFORE the infinitive (unlike conjugated verbs where they surround the verb):

Structure: ne pas + infinitive

🔴 THE "À vs. DE vs. ∅" CHALLENGE — TIPS AND PATTERNS

There is no single logical rule that determines whether a verb takes à, de, or nothing before an infinitive. However, there are helpful patterns:

Pattern 1: Modal/Ability verbs → usually ∅ (nothing)

pouvoir, vouloir, devoir, savoir, oser, faillir, sembler, paraître

Pattern 2: Beginning/Continuing verbs → usually À

commencer à, se mettre à, continuer à, s'habituer à, persister à

Pattern 3: Stopping/Ending verbs → usually DE

arrêter de, cesser de, finir de, s'arrêter de

Pattern 4: Trying/Succeeding/Failing verbs → mixed

essayer de, tenter de, BUT: réussir à, arriver à, parvenir à, chercher à

Pattern 5: Emotional reactions → usually DE

regretter de, avoir peur de, être content de, être surpris de, avoir honte de

Pattern 6: Commanding/Asking someone → usually DE

demander de, dire de, conseiller de, ordonner de, interdire de, permettre de

Pattern 7: Perception verbs → ∅ (nothing)

voir, entendre, regarder, écouter, sentir

Pattern 8: "Il est + adjective" → DE

Il est important de..., Il est difficile de..., Il est nécessaire de...

Pattern 9: "C'est + adjective" → À

C'est facile à faire, C'est difficile à comprendre, C'est bon à savoir

🔴 COMPARE these two patterns carefully:

  • Il est difficile de comprendre cette leçon. (Impersonal: It's difficult TO understand this lesson.)
  • Cette leçon est difficile à comprendre. (Personal subject: This lesson is difficult TO understand.)

The IL EST vs. C'EST Rule:

StructurePrepositionExampleEnglish
Il est + adj. + de + infinitivedeIl est important de dormir.It's important to sleep.
C'est + adj. + à + infinitiveàC'est facile à faire.It's easy to do.
Noun est + adj. + à + infinitiveàCe problème est difficile à résoudre.This problem is hard to solve.

💡 WHY the infinitif passé after après? Because après indicates that the action is COMPLETED before the next action begins. The infinitif passé (avoir/être + PP) expresses that completed state.

COMPARE with avant de:

  • Avant de manger... (Before eating...) → infinitif présent (action not yet done)
  • Après avoir mangé... (After eating...) → infinitif passé (action completed)

After avant de (before)✅❌Avant de parler... (Before speaking...)

9️⃣ TECHNIQUES & MEMORY TIPS 🧠

Technique 1: The "APRÈS = PASSÉ" Rule

APRÈS and PASSÉ both start with the idea of "after/past." So: après = infinitif passé. Always.

après AVOIR mangé    ✅
après ÊTRE parti     ✅
après S'ÊTRE levé    ✅
après manger         ❌ NEVER!

Technique 2: The "Preposition + Infinitive" Mantra

"Every preposition takes the infinitive — except EN, which takes -ANT."

sans parler     ✅ (infinitive)
avant de parler ✅ (infinitive)
pour parler     ✅ (infinitive)
au lieu de parler ✅ (infinitive)
en parlANT      ✅ (gérondif — the only exception!)

Technique 3: The "Three Buckets" System for À / DE / ∅

Organize verbs into three mental "buckets":

BUCKET ∅ (NO preposition): "I CAN, I WANT, I SEE"
  → Modal verbs: pouvoir, vouloir, devoir, savoir, falloir
  → Perception: voir, entendre, écouter, regarder, sentir
  → Movement: aller, venir, monter, descendre, courir, sortir
  → Preference: aimer, adorer, détester, préférer, souhaiter, espérer
  → Seeming: sembler, paraître
  → Making: faire, laisser

BUCKET À: "BEGINNING, LEARNING, TRYING-TO"
  → Starting: commencer à, se mettre à
  → Continuing: continuer à, s'habituer à
  → Learning: apprendre à, enseigner à
  → Succeeding: réussir à, arriver à, parvenir à
  → Helping: aider à, encourager à, inviter à
  → Hesitating: hésiter à, tarder à
  → Forcing: forcer à, obliger à, pousser à
  → Preparing: se préparer à, se décider à
  → Being ready: être prêt à, être le premier/dernier/seul à

BUCKET DE: "DECIDING, STOPPING, FEELING"
  → Deciding: décider de, choisir de
  → Stopping: arrêter de, cesser de, finir de
  → Trying: essayer de, tenter de
  → Refusing: refuser de, éviter de
  → Emotions: regretter de, avoir peur de, être content de
  → Commanding: demander de, dire de, conseiller de, interdire de
  → Promising: promettre de, proposer de, menacer de
  → Having: avoir besoin de, avoir envie de, avoir l'intention de
  → Impersonal: il est + adj. + de (il est important de...)

Technique 4: The "Négation = Both Before" Rule

For infinitive negation, remember: both negative words go BEFORE:

Conjugated verb:  Je NE parle PAS.     (surrounds verb)
Infinitive:       NE PAS parler.        (both before)
                  NE RIEN dire.         (both before)
                  NE JAMAIS mentir.     (both before)

Memory phrase: "In infinitives, negatives stick together BEFORE!"

Technique 5: The "Present = Not Yet / Future" vs. "Passé = Already Done" Test

Ask: Has the action already happened relative to the main verb?

  • NO (not yet / simultaneous / future) → Infinitif présent
  • YES (already completed) → Infinitif passé
Je veux parler.        → Speaking hasn't happened yet → présent ✅
Je regrette d'avoir parlé. → Speaking already happened → passé ✅
Avant de parler...     → Speaking hasn't happened yet → présent ✅
Après avoir parlé...   → Speaking already happened → passé ✅

Technique 6: The "Dictionary Form" Reminder

The infinitive is the form you find in the dictionary. Whenever you're unsure what the infinitive of a verb is, just think: "How would I look this up?"

je mange → manger
nous finissons → finir  
il prend → prendre
elles sont → être
j'ai → avoir

✅ USE the INFINITIF PRÉSENT for:

After verbs (vouloir, pouvoir, devoir, aimer...), after prepositions except en (sans, pour, avant de, au lieu de...), as subject of a sentence, impersonal commands (signs), deliberative questions, exclamations, with special constructions (faire + inf., laisser + inf., aller + inf., venir de + inf.)

After après (always!), expressing emotions/reactions about completed past actions (regretter d'avoir..., être content d'être...), claiming/denying past actions (affirmer avoir..., nier avoir...), after pour expressing cause for past action (puni pour avoir...), after sans for completed prior action (sans avoir compris...)

THE FINAL WORD: WHAT MAKES FRENCH TENSES MANAGEABLE

Despite having 21 tenses, French verb mastery comes down to understanding a few key principles:

Principle 1: The Compound Tense Formula

Every compound tense = a form of AVOIR or ÊTRE + past participle. Learn the past participle once, and you've unlocked 10 tenses.

Principle 2: The Two Auxiliaries

Master avoir and être in every simple tense, and you can form every compound tense automatically.

Principle 3: The Three Stems

Most verbs have three key stems: the present stem, the future/conditional stem, and the past participle. From these three, you can derive almost everything.

Principle 4: The Register Split

Modern spoken French uses about 12-14 tenses regularly. The remaining tenses are literary/archaic. You need to produce the modern ones and recognize the literary ones.

Principle 5: Patterns Over Memorization

French conjugation is full of patterns. The -ER verbs are 90% of all verbs and perfectly regular. The irregular verbs cluster into families with shared patterns. Focus on patterns, not individual memorization.
